How to Prepare a Property for Commercial Review

A practical guide for organizing commercial real estate information and asking better property questions.

Start with a clear property profile

Record the legal or commonly used address, property type, current use, approximate size, number of units, occupancy, and your relationship to the property. Distinguish facts from estimates so a reviewer knows what still needs verification.

Document current physical condition

Take recent exterior, interior, mechanical, roof-access, parking, common-area, and problem-area photos where safe and appropriate. Add short notes about known repairs, deferred maintenance, recent improvements, and any inspection reports.

Organize operating information

If the property produces income, gather available rent rolls, leases, expense summaries, utility information, tax records, insurance information, and service contracts. Remove account numbers and sensitive personal information before sharing.

Explain the goal and timing

State why the property is being submitted, what outcome is being considered, and whether any timing issue exists. A clear goal helps determine whether acquisition, disposition, management, listing, or another review pathway is most relevant.

Why Property Records Matter Before Selling

A practical guide for organizing commercial real estate information and asking better property questions.

Records create a reliable starting point

Property records help distinguish assumptions from documented facts. Deeds, tax notices, surveys, leases, permits, repair invoices, and insurance records may each answer a different part of the property story.

Ownership details can affect timing

Names on a deed, entity records, inheritance questions, liens, and other ownership matters can affect a possible transition. These issues should be reviewed by the appropriate attorney, title professional, tax professional, or other licensed advisor.

Operational records provide context

For occupied commercial property, leases, rent rolls, deposit records, expense statements, maintenance history, and vendor agreements help explain current operations. Information should be current, complete, and labeled when it is an estimate.

Protect sensitive information

Public intake forms should never contain Social Security numbers, banking credentials, passwords, or unredacted highly sensitive documents. Share only what is needed for the stage of review and use an appropriate secure process if a professional later requests more.

Understanding Acquisition, Disposition, and Management Pathways

A practical guide for organizing commercial real estate information and asking better property questions.

Acquisition review

Acquisition review considers whether a property may fit a possible purchase or investment objective. It can examine property type, location, condition, occupancy, records, operations, price context, and open questions. Review is not an offer or commitment.

Disposition review

Disposition review organizes information when an owner or family is considering a sale or other transition. It focuses on the property facts, ownership context, condition, timing, and owner goals. It is not licensed brokerage representation.

Management review

Management review focuses on ongoing operations: units, tenants, occupancy, rent information, maintenance, vendors, reporting, and owner priorities. Review does not create a management agreement or guarantee performance.

Choosing a starting point

Choose the pathway that best matches the current decision. If the situation overlaps or remains unclear, use a general property review and explain the known facts without guessing.

What Families Should Organize for Legacy Property

A practical guide for organizing commercial real estate information and asking better property questions.

Begin with known property facts

Record the address, parcel information if available, property type, condition, occupancy, utilities, insurance status, tax notices, and who currently has access. Note what is known, unknown, or disputed.

Gather documents without drawing legal conclusions

Families may locate deeds, tax bills, mortgage statements, insurance records, leases, maintenance invoices, and correspondence. These documents can support organization, but legal ownership and authority should be determined by qualified professionals.

Identify the right questions

Write down whether the family is trying to secure the property, understand expenses, identify occupants, locate records, evaluate condition, or consider a future transition. Clear questions make professional conversations more productive.

Use licensed guidance

Family First Equity Group does not provide legal, probate, estate, title, or tax advice. Families should consult appropriate attorneys, title professionals, tax professionals, and licensed advisors for those matters.

Commercial Real Estate Basics for Property Owners

A practical guide for organizing commercial real estate information and asking better property questions.

Commercial property is operational

Commercial value and risk can be connected to use, occupancy, leases, tenant quality, expenses, access, zoning, physical condition, and market context. A review should consider both the building and the operation.

Income is not the same as cash flow

Gross rent alone does not show performance. Vacancy, repairs, taxes, insurance, utilities, management, reserves, debt service, and capital work may materially change the operating picture.

Verification matters

Sizes, unit counts, lease terms, zoning, environmental conditions, title, taxes, and financial statements should be independently verified before a decision. Marketing descriptions are not substitutes for diligence.

Review differs from brokerage

An investment or property review organizes and evaluates opportunity information. Real estate brokerage involves licensed representation in transactions. Family First Equity Group is not a real estate broker or agent and does not represent buyers or sellers as one.
